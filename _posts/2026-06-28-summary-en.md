## [Suspicious Discontinuities in Distributions](https://danluu.com/discontinuities/) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Dan Luu's essay 'Suspicious Discontinuities' analyzes how artificial thresholds in data distributions reveal hidden incentives and artifacts. Understanding these discontinuities helps uncover biases and unintended consequences in systems ranging from taxes to sports. Key examples include marathon finish times rounding down, UK and Indian tax brackets creating effective marginal rates over 60%, and a skewed distribution in Polish language scores due to a 100-point cap.

hackernews · tosh · Jun 27, 13:32 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48698151)

**Background**: Discontinuities are abrupt changes in data distributions that often indicate human behavioral responses to thresholds, such as tax brackets or time targets. These patterns can be spotted through 'bunching' or 'cliff' effects in histograms.

**Discussion**: Commenters shared personal experiences, such as pushing to finish a half marathon under 2:30, and highlighted tax cliffs in UK childcare and Indian income tax rebate that create perverse incentives. Another discussion noted that marathon pace runners contribute to the clustering, and Polish language scores show a mess at the 100-point boundary.

## [Keep calm and focus on cybersecurity fundamentals after Mythos](https://cephalosec.com/blog/cybersecurity-in-the-post-mythos-era-keep-calm-and-carry-on/) ⭐️ 8.0/10

The blog post advocates a calm, fundamentals-focused approach to cybersecurity in response to the hype around Anthropic's Mythos AI model, which can exploit zero-day vulnerabilities. This perspective counters vendor fear-mongering and reminds organizations that most security issues stem from basic misconfigurations and bad practices, not advanced AI threats. It encourages a balanced understanding of AI's role in cybersecurity. The Mythos Preview, released in 2026, was found capable of identifying and exploiting zero-day vulnerabilities in major operating systems and browsers, as per evaluations by Anthropic and AISI. The blog post argues that such advanced threats should not overshadow fundamental security hygiene.
